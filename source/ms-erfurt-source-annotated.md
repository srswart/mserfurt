The current of belief is not mine. I want to say that first, before anything else, because everything that follows depends on understanding that I know this.

<!-- register: de -->
I have been a scribe for thirty years. I have copied the Word as it passed through — Psalms, Gospels, the great commentators, the minor ones — and I understood early that my role in this was what a pebble's role is in a river. The current moves. The pebble does not direct it. The pebble is carried, shaped, worn smooth in the places the water finds, and this is not a small thing, to be shaped by what passes through you, but it is not the same as choosing.

<!-- register: de -->
And yet.

<!-- register: mixed -->
<!-- hint: "the winter of 1435" stays as date; "something arrived" = personal/German; "doing penance" = mixed German/Latin theological term -->
In the winter of 1435, the current moved through my particular hand in a way it has not moved before or since. I was alone at a desk with a damaged text and a question the text could not answer for itself, and something — I will not call it inspiration, that is not mine to claim — something arrived in the specific pressure of my fingers on the quill, in the particular quality of attention I had that morning, and I made a choice that was not mine to make, and the choice was right, and I have been doing penance for it ever since.

<!-- register: de -->
I want to be honest about the penance. It is real. I do not perform it.

<!-- register: mixed -->
<!-- hint: "to You" = direct address to God, shifts toward Latin -->
I also want to be honest about the other thing, the thing beneath the penance, which I have not said to anyone and am now saying to You because You already know it and I am tired of carrying it alone:

<!-- register: de -->
I am glad it was my hand.

<!-- register: de -->
Not proud. I want that distinction to be clear, though I am aware it may not survive scrutiny. The gladness is not in the having done it. It is in the having been — for one morning, in one cold room, with one cracked manuscript — the place where the current needed to go. A pebble does not boast of the water that moved through it. But a pebble might, in its silence, know what it carried. And that knowing is what I am trying to write toward.

<!-- register: de -->
Everything else — the press, the student I lost, the eyes that are failing — is the river explaining itself to me now. I am trying to understand what I was used for before the water moves on entirely and I am left dry on the bank, which is coming, which is perhaps already here.

<!-- register: mixed -->
<!-- hint: "I have been writing to You" = address to God, Latin-inclined -->
I have been writing to You my whole life. I have never written this.

<!-- register: de -->
<!-- hint: "die neue Kunst" = already German; "ars impressoria" = Latin term of art -->
I will begin with die neue Kunst — the press, as it is beginning to be called. Not because it is where the story ends, but because it is what shook the story loose. And because approaching the harder thing directly is not yet possible. I am a scribe. I approach from the margin and work inward.

<!-- register: de -->
There is a machine now in Mainz — a machine that can produce in one day what I produce in four months. It sets letters in metal and stamps them onto the page with a force I felt in my feet when I stood next to it. I have stood next to it. I will tell you about that later. I will tell you why I told Brother Anselm I was going to the apothecary.

<!-- register: de -->
The men who operate it are devout. Several of them, I think, more devout than I am. This is the problem.

<!-- register: de -->
The pages were consistent. Letter after letter, identical, the same pressure, the same depth. A thing I have never achieved and never will achieve, not in forty more years of work. You can say this is because God made human hands to tremble, to err, to be particular — and that is what I tell myself walking home — but I stood there and I thought: whoever reads one of those pages will never know that a man made it. They will read only what it says.

<!-- register: de -->
I have spent my life leaving myself in the pages. Not obviously. Not with vanity. I mean something smaller: the slight thickening of a stroke when my attention deepened, the places where I slowed because the meaning arrived in my hands before it arrived in my mind. Readers did not see it. But it was there. The page knew.

<!-- register: de -->
Those pages did not know anything. They were perfect and they did not know anything.

<!-- register: de -->
I need certain sentences to exist.

<!-- register: de -->
When I am finished, if I am ever finished, I will probably burn this.

<!-- register: mixed -->
<!-- hint: "You are already laughing" = God-address, tends Latin; "asymmetry/faith/absurdity" = theological, mixed -->
I say that, and You are already laughing, because You know whether I burn it or not, and I don't, and that asymmetry is either the foundation of faith or the definition of absurdity and on certain mornings I cannot tell which.

<!-- register: de -->
<!-- hint: "Lauds" = Latin liturgical term kept as-is -->
It is early. The bell for Lauds has not yet rung. In two hours I will sit at my desk and continue the Psalter I am copying for a merchant in Frankfurt who does not know yet that he could have it printed for a third of the price. I will tell him eventually. I keep not telling him.

<!-- register: de -->
I think I am not ready to tell him because once I do, I will have to explain why he should still want mine.

<!-- register: de -->
I don't have that explanation yet.

<!-- register: de -->
That is also what this is for.

In the winter of 1435 I was given a commission that I understood immediately to be the most important work of my life to that point.

<!-- register: de -->
A canon in Cologne — I will not write his name, he is dead now but he has living sons — had acquired a manuscript of Meister Eckhart's German sermons and wanted a fair copy made before the original deteriorated further. The original was old, the vellum cracking at the folds, certain pages brown at the edges with the particular damage that comes from being hidden in damp places, which this manuscript had been, because Eckhart's works had been in damp places since 1329.

<!-- register: mixed -->
<!-- hint: "1329" = the condemnation; "Pope John" = German narrative with Latin papal/theological terms -->
You know what happened in 1329. You were there, in the sense that You are everywhere, though I sometimes think You were looking somewhere else that year. Pope John — a small meticulous man, from what I have read, a man who organized his theology the way a notary organizes his files — issued the bull In agro dominico and condemned twenty-eight propositions drawn from Eckhart's sermons. Eckhart himself had died the year before, which the Pope's men perhaps found convenient. The condemned propositions were things Eckhart had written or preached about the soul's relationship to God: that in its innermost ground the soul is not separate from God, that the spark of the soul is uncreated, that in the deepest act of contemplation the distinction between the soul and God becomes — Eckhart's word, not mine — nothing.
<!-- hint: "In agro dominico" = Latin, kept as-is. "the bull" = "bulla". Eckhart's propositions summarized in mixed register -->

<!-- register: mixed -->
<!-- hint: "The Church said: this is heresy" = mixed; the theological argument involves Latin terms -->
The Church said: this is heresy. The soul is created. God is uncreated. The distance between them is absolute and is bridged only by grace, by the sacraments, by the mediation of the Church. To say otherwise is to make the Church unnecessary, and the Church had noticed this.

<!-- register: mixed -->
Eckhart's defenders said: you are misreading him. He is speaking of mystical union, not ontological identity. He is a poet of the interior life, not a systematic theologian, and you are prosecuting his metaphors as if they were propositions.

<!-- register: de -->
Both of these things were true. This is the kind of situation that produces damp manuscripts.

<!-- register: de -->
I was twenty-one years old. I had been copying for ten years but I had not yet copied anything that frightened me. I did not understand, taking the commission, how different that is. You can copy a text you find dull and your hand moves across the page and the meaning slides past like water. You can copy a text you find beautiful and something in you opens and the work is a kind of joy. But when you copy a text that frightens you — when the meaning is arriving in your hand and your hand knows it is dangerous — the body responds in ways the mind hasn't authorized.

<!-- register: de -->
The sermons were extraordinary. I can say that now. At twenty-one I couldn't say it cleanly because the fear was in the way, but I knew it then and I know it now: Eckhart wrote about the interior life with a precision I have not encountered since, a precision that was not cold but was exact, the way a surgeon's incision is exact, the way it has to be exact to do the thing it needs to do. He was not using metaphor carelessly. He was using it the way the craft requires — to say the thing that cannot be said otherwise, to carry the reader to the edge of language and hold them there long enough for the non-language to do its work.

<!-- register: de -->

<!-- register: de -->

Is — heretical, condemned, the reason this manuscript was hidden.

The soul in its ground becomes the Word. Not always is. Not merely receives. Becomes, in the contemplative act, in the moment of deepest union, in the event that Eckhart was trying to describe — the soul becomes what it contemplates.
